Why we built it this way

One statement per surface, no exceptions

Most banners try to say five things. Ours say one. The difference between a roll-up that sells and a roll-up that's wallpaper is whether someone can read it from across a hall in 1.5 seconds.

  • Two roll-up variants, not one. An all-apps roll-up tells the platform story; a single-app roll-up does feature depth. Same chassis, different role at a stand.
  • Single-app roll-up has numbered features in hexes. Four hex-stamped features with one-line descriptions. Buyers scan the numbers, slow down on the hex they care about.
  • One orange hex, always at eye level. Pinned at ~110mm from the top so a 170cm visitor can read it without looking up. The hex carries the call-to-action ("conduction.nl/apps", "github.com").
  • Honeycomb backdrop in the upper third. Brand-recognisable from across a venue without competing with the headline. Lower two-thirds stay clean for content.
  • QR at the bottom-right. A LinkedIn-invite QR for visitors who want to follow up. We trade business cards for DMs at events.
  • Event backdrop says ONE word + ONE url. "Conduction." with a period, then the URL. Anything more clutters; the backdrop is the photo opportunity, not the brochure.
  • Social headers are a set, not three random images. LinkedIn = team-feel honeycomb, X = terminal-style command, Facebook = title block. Together they form a recognisable campaign across feeds.
  • Plex Mono on URLs. Every URL is in code-mono, recognisable across all formats. Reinforces the open-source identity in a glance.
