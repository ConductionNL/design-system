10 · Identity / Voice

MKB-direct. Concrete. Short.

Subject = a person, not a system. Verb = action, not existence. One claim per sentence. Less than 16 words.

Three registers · which one we use, which two we don't
Register A · the procurement voice

"De Oplossing ondersteunt het opslaan van zoekopdrachten."

Depersonalised, defensible, legally safe — built for tender documents. Subject is always "the Solution", never a human. Reads like a bestek, not a product.

"De Oplossing biedt de mogelijkheid om sjablonen inclusief huisstijl centraal te configureren."
"De Oplossing dient benaderbaar te zijn via een fully qualified domain name."
Use? No. Reserved for documentation and detailed feature lists on app-detail pages.
Register B · the requirements voice

"Het systeem moet alle intrekkingen opslaan."

Functional requirements, imperative, testable. Subject is "the system". One step further into robot territory than register A — and twice as anonymous.

"Het systeem moet een controletrail bijhouden van alle kostentoewijzingen."
"Het systeem moet de afhankelijkheidsrelatie tussen lastenorders en invorderingsbeschikkingen registreren."
Use? No. Focuses on what the system does, not what the user achieves.
Register C · the user-story voice

"Als gebruiker wil ik mijn zoekopdrachten opslaan, zodat ik ze later opnieuw kan gebruiken."

A real person, a concrete goal, a motivation. With one tweak — drop the "Als gebruiker" wrapper — it lands exactly on Conduction's tone.

"Als gebruiker wil ik filtering op content, zodat ik enkel relevante content tot mijn beschikking heb."
"Als gebruiker wil ik tickets toewijzen, zodat ik gericht aan een collega kan overdragen."
Use? Yes — but rewrite shorter. Address the reader directly. See recipes below.

The seven rules · keep these in your head
1
Subject = a person, not a system.

"Je" · "jij" · "we" beats "de Oplossing" · "het systeem" every time. Even when the system does the work, write it from the user's side.

2
Verb = action, not existence.

"Genereer" beats "heeft de mogelijkheid te genereren". "Exporteer" beats "biedt export-functionaliteit". Active voice, no nominalisation.

3
Result before motivation.

User stories open with "Als X wil ik Y zodat Z" — flip it: "Y — zodat Z." Or just "Y. Z."

4
Less than 16 words.

If the sentence runs past 16, split it. Two short sentences read faster than one long one — and they make every claim defensible on its own.

5
Jargon needs context.

WOO-coördinator? Fine, but explain it the first time. SAML? OK in tech-context. Ketensamenwerking? Never.

6
Acronyms: spell out first, abbreviate after.

"Wet open overheid (Woo)" on first mention. After that, "Woo" alone. Don't make readers Google.

7
One claim per sentence.

Marketing sentences with multiple claims ("snel, schaalbaar, veilig en betrouwbaar") get skipped. One claim, one sentence — the reader can verify each independently.

Banned phrases · the marketing-noise list

Words that show up in tenders and don't show up on our site.

Each one is a sign that a sentence is hiding behind generic language. Strip the word and check whether the sentence still works. If it does — the word was noise.
