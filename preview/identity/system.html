Why these colours, this type, this hex.

Every system choice has a reason. Read this when something feels arbitrary — it isn't.

Colour · why these and not the obvious alternatives
Primary

Cobalt blue #21468B

The Dutch flag's official blue — heavier than legacy SaaS blues, lighter than bank navy. It signals trustworthy and Dutch without being either nostalgic or government-grey. Fills 20% of any surface — the proportion is the rule, not just the hex.

Considered: Standard navy (#1E3A8A — too SaaS-tech-blue) · Inter-blue (#3B82F6 — too generic). Cobalt has Dutch identity built in.
Accent · 8% rule

KNVB orange #F36C21

The Dutch national football team's orange — warm, instantly readable as Dutch, but not Rijkshuisstijl-orange (which would pull us into government-document territory). Capped at ~8% of any surface. Never a primary fill, never an interactive button background.

Considered: Rijkshuisstijl orange (#CB571B — too gemeente). Pantone Reflex Orange (#FF5800 — too loud). KNVB sits at the warm-Dutch sweet spot.
Tertiary · sparingly

Vermillion red #AE1C28

The Dutch flag's red. Rare — used for status (errors, deletions, banned-phrase callouts) and for the rare moment we want to invoke flag-energy without leaning on orange. Capped at ~2% of any surface.

Considered: Standard error-red (#DC2626 — generic). Vermillion has Dutch flag-warmth without being aggressive.
Guest · ConNext context only

Nextcloud blue #0082C9

Nextcloud's official blue. A guest colour in our system — used only when the context is ConNext-specific: the wordmark's "Next", the kernel hex on platform diagrams, brand citations in copy. Never on functional "next" (next page, next year).

Rule: If you're styling a generic UI element, this isn't your colour. If you're citing Nextcloud the platform — it is.

Type · Figtree + IBM Plex Mono
Figtree · headings & body Round, calm, humanist sans-serif 400 · 500 · 600 · 700
Body + heading

Figtree

Round terminals, neutral character, broad weight range. Reads calm, not corporate-cold. Open Font License (OFL) so it's bundleable. Works at 12px through display sizes without splitting personality.

Considered: Inter (too SaaS, terminals too pointy). Roboto (too Google). Manrope (too geometric). Figtree's roundness gives the brand its calm.
$ cookies --reconfigure # IBM Plex Mono → for code, eyebrows, meta
Code + meta

IBM Plex Mono

Generous x-height, readable at small sizes, has personality without being cute. Used for code blocks, eyebrows ("THE PLATFORM OVERVIEW"), code-style metadata, and our cookie-CLI prompt. OFL-licensed.

Considered: JetBrains Mono (slightly too techy). Source Code Pro (too neutral). Plex Mono has the right blend of authority + warmth.
Aa 9-step ramp · 12 → 48px + display
Why no display font

One family, more weights.

We don't ship a separate display font. Figtree at 700 with negative letter-spacing handles every headline up to 64px without splitting into a second typeface. Less to load, more consistency, fewer foundry-licence questions.

Decision: A display font is a second brand voice. We have one. Figtree carries the whole ramp.
No ligatures No alternates No discretionary stylistic sets
Type discipline

OpenType features off by default.

Ligatures, swashes, alternates — all off. Why? They render inconsistently across platforms (especially WCAG screen readers + older PDFs), and they pull the eye in ways that hurt scannability. Web-rendered Figtree always reads as Figtree.

Exception: We turn on tabular-nums for numeric tables. Otherwise, font-feature-settings: "liga" 0; at the root.
Hex motif · always pointy-top, never flat-top
POINTY-TOP · the only hex we ship
The shape rule

Pointy-top, every time.

No flat-top variants anywhere in the brand. The pointy-top hex carries through from app icons to bullets to status badges to pagination — anywhere a shape means something. Functional containers (buttons, inputs, cards) stay rectangular. Hexagons indicate. Rectangles do.

Why not flat-top? Flat-top hexes read as honeycomb-cells, food-tech, biology — wrong industry signal. Pointy-top reads as crystal, modular system, deliberate construction.

The hex as system

Bullets, badges, avatars, dividers.

A logo wrapper is just the start. The hex shows up as the bullet on every list, the status indicator next to every app, the avatar wrapper for every team member, the divider between sections, the timeline bead in step diagrams. Anywhere a shape means something, the hex is the shape.

Where it doesn't go: buttons, inputs, cards, modals, tables. Rectangles do the work; hexes do the indicating.
Flat-isometric hex-prism style for all illustrations. No people, no faces, no characters. No stock photos, no 3D renders, no gradient backgrounds (except the Nextcloud-blue gradient on the kernel hex).
Illustration direction

Flat-isometric hex-prism. Reference: Honeycomb.io.

Hex-prisms — same pointy-top hex extruded with a slight isometric depth, flat shading, no Pixar lighting. Every illustration in the system uses this geometry, with pastel category palettes per face. Reference: Honeycomb's platform-overview diagram.

What we don't use: photography, 3D renders, mascot characters (OpenPeeps, Tom Froese-style figures), gradient backgrounds, neon lighting. The geometry carries the visual; we don't supplement it.
